The Modern Cinematic Glossary
Framing
How subjects are arranged within the edges of the screen to create meaning or balance.
Montage
A series of short clips edited together to show the passage of time or development.
Blocking
The careful arrangement of actors and movement within a scene by the director.
Jump Cut
An abrupt cut that creates a jarring effect, often used to show a time skip.
Boom Mic
A microphone mounted on a long pole used to capture sound while staying out of frame.
Match Cut
A seamless transition between two similar visual elements or actions across two shots.
Soft Focus
A slight blurring of the image to create a dreamy or romantic atmosphere.
Rack Focus
A shift in focus during a shot to draw attention from one subject to another.
Chiaroscuro
A lighting technique that uses strong contrasts between light and dark to create dramatic mood.
Dutch Angle
A tilted camera shot that creates a sense of unease or disorientation in the viewer.
Tracking Shot
A smooth camera movement that follows a subject through the scene.
Practical Lighting
Using real light sources visible in the scene, like lamps or candles.
Extreme Close-Up
A shot that tightly frames a subject, focusing on fine details like eyes or hands.
Over-the-Shoulder Shot
A perspective shot from behind a character's shoulder, typically used during conversations.

The Modern Cinematic Glossary
Tilt
A vertical camera movement up or down on a fixed base.
Pan
A horizontal camera movement from a static position.
Zoom
Changing the camera lens to magnify or shrink the subject within the frame.
Cutaway
A brief shot that interrupts the main action to show supporting detail.
Whip Pan
A fast horizontal camera movement used for dynamic transitions or to reveal surprises.
Voiceover
Narration by a character or narrator that plays over the visual content.
Low Angle
A shot from below looking up, making the subject appear powerful or dominant.
Wide Shot
Captures the entire subject within the frame and emphasizes setting or scale.
Insert Shot
A close-up of an object to draw attention to a detail.
High Angle
A shot from above looking down, often used to make subjects appear vulnerable.
Reverse Shot
Used in dialogue scenes to switch perspective between speakers.
Handheld Shot
A shaky camera style that adds realism or urgency.
Eye-Level Shot
The camera is positioned at the subject's eye level, creating a neutral perspective.
Establishing Shot
A wide shot at the start of a scene that provides context or setting.

The Modern Cinematic Glossary
Cameo
A brief appearance of a well-known figure in a minor role.
Close-Up
A tightly framed shot of a subject, usually a face, to show emotion.
Crane Shot
A high moving shot from a crane, often used to swoop over a scene.
Voice Match
Re-recording dialogue to match the original delivery, often used in post-production.
Medium Shot
Shows a character from the waist up, balancing emotion and context.
Green Screen
A background replacement technique using chroma key effects.
Cross-Cutting
Editing between different locations to show parallel action.
Depth of Field
The range of distance within a shot that appears acceptably sharp.
Color Grading
The process of altering and enhancing the color of a film during editing.
Overexposure
An image that is too bright due to too much light hitting the camera sensor.
Diegetic Sound
Sound that originates from the world of the film, like dialogue or footsteps.
Underexposure
An image that is too dark due to too little light being captured.
Ambient Sound
Background noise that sets the scene's atmosphere.
Non-Diegetic Sound
Sound not heard by characters, such as a music score or narrator.

The Modern Cinematic Glossary
J Cut
When audio from the next scene begins before the image appears.
Foley
Custom-recorded sound effects added in post-production to enhance realism.
L Cut
When the audio from a previous scene continues into the next visual scene.
Two-Shot
A frame that includes two characters, often used in conversation scenes.
Storyboard
A sequence of drawings that plan out each shot of a film.
Overcranking
Recording at a higher frame rate, which when played at normal speed, results in slow motion.
Undercranking
Recording at a lower frame rate to create a fast-motion effect.
Point of View (POV) Shot
Simulates the vision of a character, putting the viewer in their shoes.
